Select Features & Scale
Plain English We'll cluster on 2 features: Income and Spending Score. K-Means measures straight-line distance between customers — income ranges 15-137, spending score ranges 1-99. Without scaling, income dominates every distance calculation unfairly.
python
X = df[["Annual Income (k$)", "Spending Score (1-100)"]].values

scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)

print("Before scaling:", X[0])
print("After  scaling:", X_scaled[0].round(3))
Output
Before scaling: [15 39]
After scaling: [-1.734 -0.434]
Scaling is not optional Exactly the same rule as KNN — K-Means is 100% distance-based. Skip scaling and your clusters will be dominated by whichever feature happens to have bigger raw numbers.
StandardScaler required distance-based algorithm

3
Watch K-Means Converge — Interactive
Plain English Click "Step" to watch K-Means work, one iteration at a time. Points get assigned to their nearest centroid (colored), then each centroid moves to the average position of its group (X marks). Repeat until nothing moves anymore.
Iteration: 0 (random start)
Watch what happens Iteration 0: centroids are random, assignments look messy. By iteration 3-5, the clusters usually stabilize completely — points stop switching groups and centroids stop moving. That's convergence.
assign → recalculate → repeat convergence
Step 3 of 8
4
Find the Best K
Plain English We don't know how many natural groups exist ahead of time. We test K=2 through K=10 and track two signals: inertia (how tight each cluster is) and silhouette score (how well-separated clusters are). We use silhouette score to pick the winner — more reliable than inertia alone.
python
inertias, sil_scores = [], []
K_range = range(2, 11)

for k in K_range:
    km = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=42, n_init=10)
    labels = km.fit_predict(X_scaled)
    inertias.append(km.inertia_)
    sil_scores.append(silhouette_score(X_scaled, labels))
    print(f"K={k}  inertia={km.inertia_:.2f}  silhouette={sil_scores[-1]:.4f}")

best_k = list(K_range)[int(np.argmax(sil_scores))]
print(f"\nBest K: {best_k}")
Output
K=2   inertia=269.69   silhouette=0.3213
K=3   inertia=157.70   silhouette=0.4666
K=4   inertia=108.92   silhouette=0.4939
K=5   inertia=65.57    silhouette=0.5547  ← best
K=6   inertia=55.06    silhouette=0.5399
K=7   inertia=44.86    silhouette=0.5281
...

Best K: 5
Why K=5 and not K=10? Silhouette score peaks exactly at K=5, then declines. Inertia keeps dropping as K grows (it always does), but the elbow — the point where it stops dropping fast — also lands right around K=5. Both signals agree.
n_init=10 silhouette_score best K=5
Step 4 of 8
5
Train the Final Model
Plain English Now we train with K=5. n_init=10 runs the whole algorithm 10 times with different random starting centroids and keeps the best result — this avoids getting unlucky with a bad random start.
python
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=5, random_state=42, n_init=10)
cluster_labels = kmeans.fit_predict(X_scaled)

df["Cluster"] = cluster_labels

print(f"Final inertia: {kmeans.inertia_:.2f}")
print("Customers per cluster:")
print(df["Cluster"].value_counts().sort_index())
Output

Step 5 of 8
6
Interpret the Clusters
Plain English K-Means gives you cluster NUMBERS — 0, 1, 2, 3, 4. It has no idea what they mean. That's our job. We look at the average income and spending score in each cluster and give them human names based on the pattern.
python
summary = df.groupby("Cluster")[
    ["Annual Income (k$)", "Spending Score (1-100)"]
].mean().round(1)
summary["Count"] = df["Cluster"].value_counts().sort_index()
print(summary)

Classify a New Customer

python
new_customer = np.array([[75, 85]])  # income=75k, spending=85
new_customer_scaled = scaler.transform(new_customer)

predicted_cluster = kmeans.predict(new_customer_scaled)[0]

No confidence score — and that's normal Unlike classifiers, K-Means doesn't say "73% confident." It simply picks whichever centroid is nearest. If you need a confidence-like measure, you can look at the distance to the nearest vs second-nearest centroid — closer together means more ambiguous.
